\subsection{Forward Chaining (Maintaining Arc Consistency)}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Idea and Architecture}

\paragraph{Why MAC instead of pure Forward Chaining or DPLL}~\\
Pure Forward Chaining (FC) starts from known facts and applies Modus Ponens
exhaustively until no new fact can be derived.  For Futoshiki puzzles with
few given clues, FC alone terminates with an \emph{incomplete} assignment:
many cells still have multiple candidate values and no rule fires to resolve
them.  The solver is stuck without guessing.

DPLL (Davis–Putnam–Logemann–Loveland), on the other hand, operates on a
\emph{propositional CNF} encoding.  Every cell-value pair becomes a Boolean
variable; the KB is a flat list of clauses.  Unit Propagation plays the role
of Forward Chaining at the Boolean level.  While correct, the CNF
representation discards the structure of the problem: a ``domain'' is
implicit across $N$ Boolean variables per cell and must be reconstructed
during branching.

\textbf{MAC (Maintaining Arc Consistency)} retains the native \emph{domain}
representation — each cell $(i,j)$ holds a set of candidate values
$D_{i,j} \subseteq \{1,\ldots,N\}$ — and interleaves FC-style propagation
with backtracking search.  Every time the search makes a tentative
assignment, FC is re-run on the updated domain to prune inconsistent values
from neighbouring cells before recursing deeper.  This \emph{maintains} arc
consistency throughout the search, detecting dead ends far earlier than
either pure FC or DPLL.

Concretely, the implementation is organised in two tiers:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Inference engine (\texttt{\_run\_fc}):}  A FOL Forward
    Chaining engine that processes an agenda of typed facts
    $\{$\texttt{Assign}$(i,j,v)$, \texttt{Remove}$(i,j,v)\}$
    and applies five Modus Ponens rules (described below) until the agenda is
    empty or a contradiction is detected.

    \item \textbf{Search controller (\texttt{\_backtrack}):}  When FC cannot
    reduce any domain further, a branching step selects the most constrained
    unassigned cell (MRV), guesses a value, invokes FC, and recurses.
    On failure, the domain snapshot is restored and the next value is tried.
\end{itemize}

The key insight is that \texttt{\_run\_fc} is called \emph{both} as an
initial propagation pass over the puzzle clues and as the consistency-
maintenance step after every branching decision — the same code plays both
roles, which is the defining characteristic of MAC.

\subsubsection{Algorithm Analysis}

\paragraph{Domain Representation}~\\
Each cell $(i,j)$ maintains a set $D_{i,j} \subseteq \{1,\ldots,N\}$.
A cell is considered \emph{assigned} when $|D_{i,j}| = 1$.
The initial state sets $D_{i,j} = \{1,\ldots,N\}$ for every cell.

\paragraph{Initial Facts}~\\
The puzzle instance is converted into a seed agenda of typed facts:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\text{Given}(i,j,v) \;\Rightarrow\; \texttt{Assign}(i,j,v)$
    \item $\text{LessH}(i,j):\; (i,j) < (i,j{+}1) \;\Rightarrow\;
          \texttt{Remove}(i,j,N)$ and $\texttt{Remove}(i,j{+}1,1)$
    \item $\text{GreaterH}(i,j):\; (i,j) > (i,j{+}1) \;\Rightarrow\;
          \texttt{Remove}(i,j,1)$ and $\texttt{Remove}(i,j{+}1,N)$
    \item Similarly for vertical constraints $\text{LessV}$ and
          $\text{GreaterV}$.
\end{itemize}
These correspond directly to the ground instances of FOL axioms A4--A9.

\paragraph{Five Modus Ponens Rules (FC Engine)}~\\
The FC engine applies the following rules on each fact popped from the agenda:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\hline
\textbf{Rule} & \textbf{Trigger} & \textbf{Effect} \\
\hline
R1 & $\texttt{Assign}(i,j,v)$ arrived, $v \notin D_{i,j}$ &
     Contradiction $\Rightarrow$ return \textit{False} \\
R2 & $\texttt{Remove}(i,j,v)$ makes $D_{i,j} = \emptyset$ &
     Contradiction $\Rightarrow$ return \textit{False} \\
R3 & $\texttt{Assign}(i,j,v)$ &
     Push $\texttt{Remove}(i,c,v)$ for all $c \neq j$ (row) \\
   & & Push $\texttt{Remove}(r,j,v)$ for all $r \neq i$ (column) \\
R4 & $|D_{i,j}| = 1$ after a Remove &
     Push $\texttt{Assign}(i,j,v')$ where $v'$ is the sole survivor \\
R5 & Any domain change at $(i,j)$ &
     Propagate new $\min/\max$ of $D_{i,j}$ to all four inequality neighbours \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Rules R1--R4 correspond to the grounded FOL axioms A1--A3 (cell uniqueness
and row/column permutation).  Rule R5 implements A4--A9 (inequality
constraints): if $(i,j) < (i,j{+}1)$, then every value $v \leq
\min(D_{i,j})$ is infeasible for $(i,j{+}1)$ and is pushed as a
\texttt{Remove} fact.

\paragraph{Backtracking with MRV}~\\
When the FC engine returns without fully assigning all cells, the search
controller:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Selects the unassigned cell with the \emph{minimum remaining
    values} (MRV) in its domain — the fail-first heuristic.
    \item Takes a snapshot of the entire domain matrix.
    \item Tries each value $v \in D_{i,j}$ in ascending order.  For each
    attempt, it pushes $\texttt{Assign}(i,j,v)$ onto a fresh agenda and
    calls \texttt{\_run\_fc}.
    \item On success, recurses.  On failure (FC returns \textit{False} or
    the recursive call fails), restores the snapshot and tries the next $v$.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Complexity Analysis}~

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llp{5.5cm}}
\hline
\textbf{Aspect} & \textbf{Bound} & \textbf{Remark} \\
\hline
Domain size & $O(N^3)$ & $N^2$ cells, each domain $\leq N$ \\
FC per node & $O(N^3)$ & Each value removed at most once \\
MRV selection & $O(N^2)$ & Scan all cells; early exit at size 2 \\
Max search depth & $\leq N^2$ & One cell assigned per level \\
Backtracks (worst) & $O(N! \,)$ & Full combinatorial space \\
Backtracks (typical) & $O(N^2)$ & MRV heuristic combined with arc consistency eliminates combinatorial explosion on large inputs \\
Space per snapshot & $O(N^3)$ & Copy of all domain sets \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Advantages and Limitations}~
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stronger pruning than DPLL:} MAC propagates inequality
    constraints through min/max bounds on domain sets, a finer-grained
    operation than Boolean unit propagation. Infeasible branches are
    detected several levels earlier. This lightweight micro-operation 
    allows MAC to process tens of thousands of nodes in fractions of 
    a second, as detailed in the Experiments section.

    \item \textbf{Directly mirrors FOL semantics:} Facts and rules are typed
    (Assign / Remove) and match the FOL predicates $Val(i,j,v)$,
    $\text{LessH}$, $\text{GreaterH}$, etc.\ without a translation layer.

    \item \textbf{Complete:} Backtracking guarantees a solution is found
    whenever one exists.

    \item \textbf{No clause learning:} Unlike CDCL-based SAT solvers, this
    implementation does not record conflict clauses.  Repeated conflicts in
    structurally similar sub-trees are re-explored.

    \item \textbf{Snapshot cost:} Each backtracking node copies the full
    $O(N^3)$ domain matrix.  For $N = 9$ this is manageable ($\approx 729$
    integers) but grows rapidly with $N$.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Pseudocode}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{MAC Master Coordinator}
\label{alg:mac-solve}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Function{solve}{\textit{puzzle}}
    \State $D \gets$ initialise all domains to $\{1,\ldots,N\}$
    \State $\mathit{facts}_0 \gets \Call{InitialFacts}{\mathit{puzzle}}$
    \Comment{Given clues + boundary inequalities}
    \If{\textbf{not} $\Call{RunFC}{D,\,\mathit{facts}_0}$}
        \State \Return $\mathit{None}$ \Comment{Contradiction in initial facts}
    \EndIf
    \If{$\Call{IsSolved}{D}$}
        \State \Return $\Call{ExtractGrid}{D}$
    \EndIf
    \If{$\Call{Backtrack}{D}$}
        \State \Return $\Call{ExtractGrid}{D}$
    \EndIf
    \State \Return $\mathit{None}$
\EndFunction
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{FC Engine --- Modus Ponens on Domain Facts}
\label{alg:mac-fc}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Function{RunFC}{$D$, $\mathit{facts}$}
    \State $\mathit{agenda} \gets \Call{deque}{\mathit{facts}}$;
           $\;\mathit{seen} \gets \{\mathit{facts}\}$;
           $\;\mathit{assigned} \gets \emptyset$
    \While{$\mathit{agenda}$ is not empty}
        \State $\mathit{fact} \gets \mathit{agenda}.\Call{popleft}{}$
        \If{$\mathit{fact} = \texttt{Assign}(i,j,v)$}
            \If{$v \notin D[i][j]$} \Return $\mathit{False}$
            \Comment{R1: contradiction}
            \EndIf
            \If{$(i,j) \notin \mathit{assigned}$}
                \State $D[i][j] \gets \{v\}$;
                       $\;\mathit{assigned}.\Call{add}{(i,j)}$
                \For{$c \neq j$} \Call{Push}{$\texttt{Remove}(i,c,v)$}
                \EndFor
                \Comment{R3: row}
                \For{$r \neq i$} \Call{Push}{$\texttt{Remove}(r,j,v)$}
                \EndFor
                \Comment{R3: column}
                \State $\Call{PropagateIneq}{i, j, D, \mathit{Push}}$
                \Comment{R5}
            \EndIf
        \ElsIf{$\mathit{fact} = \texttt{Remove}(i,j,v)$}
            \If{$v \notin D[i][j]$} \textbf{continue} \EndIf
            \State $D[i][j].\Call{discard}{v}$
            \If{$D[i][j] = \emptyset$} \Return $\mathit{False}$
            \Comment{R2: contradiction}
            \EndIf
            \If{$|D[i][j]| = 1$}
                \State \Call{Push}{$\texttt{Assign}(i,j,\text{sole value of } D[i][j])$}
                \Comment{R4}
            \EndIf
            \State $\Call{PropagateIneq}{i, j, D, \mathit{Push}}$
            \Comment{R5}
        \EndIf
    \EndWhile
    \State \Return $\mathit{True}$
\EndFunction
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Inequality Propagation (Rule R5)}
\label{alg:mac-ineq}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Function{PropagateIneq}{$i,j,D,\mathit{push}$}
    \State $\mathit{lo} \gets \min(D[i][j])$;
           $\;\mathit{hi} \gets \max(D[i][j])$
    \For{each neighbour $(i',j')$ of $(i,j)$ connected by an inequality}
        \If{$(i,j) < (i',j')$}
            \Comment{$(i,j)$ is the smaller side}
            \For{$v \in D[i'][j']$ with $v \leq \mathit{lo}$}
                \State $\mathit{push}(\texttt{Remove}(i',j',v))$
            \EndFor
        \ElsIf{$(i,j) > (i',j')$}
            \Comment{$(i,j)$ is the larger side}
            \For{$v \in D[i'][j']$ with $v \geq \mathit{hi}$}
                \State $\mathit{push}(\texttt{Remove}(i',j',v))$
            \EndFor
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
\EndFunction
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{MAC Backtracking with MRV}
\label{alg:mac-bt}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Function{Backtrack}{$D$}
    \State $(i,j) \gets \Call{MRV}{D}$
    \Comment{Cell with smallest $|D[i][j]| > 1$; \textbf{None} if all assigned}
    \If{$(i,j) = \mathit{None}$} \Return $\mathit{True}$ \EndIf
    \For{$v \in \Call{sorted}{D[i][j]}$}
        \State $D_{\mathit{saved}} \gets \Call{DeepCopy}{D}$
        \If{$\Call{RunFC}{D,\,[\texttt{Assign}(i,j,v)]}$}
            \If{$\Call{Backtrack}{D}$} \Return $\mathit{True}$ \EndIf
        \EndIf
        \State $D \gets D_{\mathit{saved}}$
        \Comment{Restore snapshot}
    \EndFor
    \State \Return $\mathit{False}$
\EndFunction
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Illustration}

Consider a $4 \times 4$ Futoshiki puzzle with the following initial state: cell $(0,0)$ is given as 2, cell $(3,3)$ is given as 4, and there is a single inequality constraint $(0,1) > (0,2)$. The initial agenda is populated with these base facts.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{FC pass 1 (Initial Propagation):}
    The \texttt{Assign}$(0,0,2)$ and \texttt{Assign}$(3,3,4)$ facts are processed.
    Rule R3 pushes \texttt{Remove} facts to eliminate 2 from row 0 and column 0, and 4 from row 3 and column 3. 
    The inequality $(0,1) > (0,2)$ triggers Rule R5, pushing \texttt{Remove}$(0,1,1)$ (as the larger cell must be strictly greater than the absolute minimum 1) and \texttt{Remove}$(0,2,4)$ (as the smaller cell must be strictly less than the absolute maximum 4).
    As the domain $D[0,1]$ loses values 1 (due to the inequality) and 2 (due to row 0), its remaining domain becomes $\{3,4\}$. The domain $D[0,2]$ loses 2 and 4, narrowing down to $\{1,3\}$.

    \item \textbf{FC Stuck:} 
    The agenda eventually becomes empty. Several cells still have $|D| > 1$. The \texttt{IsSolved} function evaluates to \textit{False}, meaning the pure deduction phase has halted without a complete assignment.

    \item \textbf{Backtracking (MRV) and Cascade:}
    The search controller selects the unassigned cell with the Minimum Remaining Values. Suppose it picks $(0,1)$ with $D = \{3,4\}$.
    It first guesses $v=3$ by pushing \texttt{Assign}$(0,1,3)$ into a fresh agenda and invoking \texttt{RunFC}.
    This triggers Rule R3, pushing \texttt{Remove}$(0,2,3)$ to eliminate 3 from the rest of row 0.
    The domain $D[0,2]$ (which was previously $\{1,3\}$) loses 3, reducing its size to exactly 1 ($D[0,2] = \{1\}$).
    Rule R4 immediately fires, pushing \texttt{Assign}$(0,2,1)$.
    Concurrently, Rule R5 propagates the new $\max(D[0,1]) = 3$, enforcing that $(0,2)$ must be $< 3$, which perfectly aligns with the new singleton domain.
    This single heuristic guess creates a powerful chain reaction (cascade) that rapidly collapses the remaining domains, successfully finding the solution without needing further backtracks.
\end{enumerate}

The total number of backtracks on this instance is typically zero or one,
because arc consistency eliminates almost all ambiguity after the first FC
pass.
